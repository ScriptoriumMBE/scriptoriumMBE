# A proposed nomenclature for quartal harmony

> [!Note] This is a work in progress, though it is already readable in its current form.

Quartal chords are chords built by stacking fourths on top of one another. Just like common chords in tonal are built by stacking thirds. We will use the square □ and triangle △ symbols to symbolise each, respectively. We will proceed analogising the system of chord naming used for △-chords.

Fourths can be of different species. The ordinary species are perfect (or just) and augmented (the tritone). The fourth can also be diminished (enharmonic with a major third).

We will currently focus only on quartal chords made from perfect and augmented fourths (diminished fourths will be included on a future version of this article).

There are diatonic (following the so-called greek modes) and chromatic quartal chords. One of our approaches (the *jazzier* one) will name quartal chords by the mode in which they appear as the tonic-quartal-chord (and indicating alterations from the mode by accidentals).
The other approach will name them indicating where are the tritones located using a subscript.
To indicate the amount of notes in a chord, we will use a superscript.

The abbreviations are to be read like this:
J→Just (also might be symbolised by lcr and called Locrian, specially when it has more notes). It might be read aloud as "Perfect-fourths stack".
Ion→Ionian
Lyd→Lydian
Mix→Mixolydian
Dor→Dorian
Eol→Eolian
Phr→Phrygian;
the above can also be abbreviated with just the first letter.


m-→meta-
L-d→Lydian-dominant

fractions symbolise polychords.


Do notice that tritones can only occur intercalated with perfect fourths, as two tritones make an octave. Perfect fourths, on the other hand, can be stacked on top of another 12 times before returning to the original pitch.

I discovered the meta-Lydian and meta-Ionian chords, which are also dodecaphonic tone-rows. I have used these chords in my pieces, like the [[Music/Partita para viola (2024-26)\|Partita for viola]]. They are built by alternating just and augmented fourths (just like the meta-Lydian-△-chord is built by alternating major and minor thirds).

### Examples

**La medianoche**
![La Medianoche (M B-E) ej. cc. 1-4.png|400](/img/user/other_files/La%20Medianoche%20(M%20B-E)%20ej.%20cc.%201-4.png)
This four-bar loop starts my song "La medianoche", a poem by Gabriela Mistral. The harmony can be cyphered like this:
$$E♭□⁴Lyd — Fm9 — G♭maj7 — D♭maj7$$
or, with a little more redundancy (which could be useful on a piece that changes greatly between different interval-based chord systems): $$□\space E♭⁴Lyd — △Fm9 — △G♭maj7 — △D♭maj7$$
Notice how, without an accurate, precise nomenclature for quartal chords, we could perfectly describe the chords and harmony in the last three chords (as they come from triadic harmony, that has many different styles of accurate cyphering), but we couldn't do the same with the first.

**The Rite of Spring**
![The Rite of Spring ex. aprox 16-18.png|600](/img/user/other_files/The%20Rite%20of%20Spring%20ex.%20aprox%2016-18.png)
This fragment of the two-piano-reduction *The Rite of Spring* by Stravinsky (between rehearsal numbers 16 and 18 of the orchestral version) mixes different harmonic systems in layers. In analysing bar 1-2 and 7-8 of the image, we can now describe accurately the chords of the first piano player, without risking confusion with quartal or quintal chords that include tritones. The chords are $\large⬠⁴J$ and $\large□⁶J$.
